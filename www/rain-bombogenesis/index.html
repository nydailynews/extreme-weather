By KELLI R. PARKER

This article is adapted from our snowstorm-specific bombogenesis article.

The bombogenesis is at hand, again. If this sounds familiar, that's because it is.

What's that? You have no idea what that means? Well, you're not alone. Bombogenesis — a fancy term for a winter hurricane — can happen when a cold air mass collides with a warm air mass, like air over warm ocean water. The formation of the rapidly strengthening weather system is what creates a bomb cyclone.

Got all that? Great. But what does all this mean for us this time around? In short, the day is going to be wet, windy and cold.

According to The Weather Channel, the current storm has the potential to undergo bombogenesis as well. But even if it doesn’t, New York will still feel its effects: big winds, icy rain, snow, flooded roads, flight cancellations and a dangerous commute.

Unlike last time, we're not expected to get piles of snow, according to the National Weather Service. Just two to four inches. High winds, heavy rain and coastal flooding are much more of a concern.
